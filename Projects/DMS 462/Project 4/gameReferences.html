Game: Catan

Designer: Klaus Teuber

Released: 1995

Description:
“Players try to be the dominant force on the island of Catan by building settlements, cities, and roads. On each turn dice are rolled to determine what resources the island produces. Players collect these resources (cards)—wood, grain, brick, sheep, or stone—to build up their civilizations to get to 10 victory points and win the game.”

Chapter 3:
Invitation to Play:
The game is very simple in its design. Wooden pieces and a number of cards with pictures representing different resources can be found within the game’s box. These simple elements are easy to understand for beginners and thus very inviting to all kinds of players.

Number of players: 3-4

Player roles:
Roles are uniform for all players. Each player has the goal to accumulate 10 victory points. They can, however, accomplish this goal in a number of different ways.

Player Interaction Pattern:
  • Multilateral

Objectives:
  • Main objective to obtain 10 victory points
  • This can be done in a few ways, including:
    • Players can purchase “Development Cards” which may or may not award them with victory points
    • Players can strive to build the longest road, which will get them some more victory points
    • Players can also attempt to build as many settlements and cities as they can, each awarding them with more victory points

Procedures:
  • Placing board tiles
    • This adds a bit of randomness to the game. Although there is a recommended board set up, you can still change it up if you want.
  • Roll the dice
  • Collect resources
  • Build roads/settlements/cities
  • Move the robber
  • Trade (with the bank and with other players)

Resources:
  • Wood
  • Grain
  • Brick
  • Sheep
  • Stone

Sources of conflict:
  • Players can move the robber wherever they want, causing a resource to be blocked until it is moved again
  • Players may attempt to “block” other players from accomplishing certain goals. For example, a player may try to prevent another from building the longest road by blocking their path with their own road
  • Resources are limited
  • A player can, but shouldn’t, have more than 7 cards in their hand, otherwise they risk losing half of their resources

Boundaries:
  • Finite resources
  • Finite space to build roads/settlements/cities
  • Unwritten agreement between players to follow the rules and procedures

Possible outcomes:
  • A player wins the game
  • The rest of the players lose

Describe how you might change the elements of the system in order to significantly change the dynamics; what do you believe the new dynamics will be like? Will the game still be playable? Will it still be balanced?
One way in which to change the dynamics of the game system significantly would be to remove the limit placed on the number of cards you can hold. This would also therefore eliminate the rule that if a player rolls a 7, every player with more than 7 cards must get rid of half. This would change the dynamics of the system by effectively making the finite amount of resource cards very scarce. This would happen because players would more than likely hoard resources because there is no repercussion for doing so. This may, as a result, increase trading among players: If one player gets to a point where they have all of a particular type of resource, other players would want to trade with that player to get the resource they need. Although this version of the game would still be playable, it would more than likely be very unbalanced. If one player happens to position his pieces in a way such that he has a monopoly on a particular resource, all other players become dependent on that player to get the resources they need.

Describe the economy within the game (if there is one). Is there a metaeconomy?
There is a simple bartering system in Catan, where players may either trade resources amongst each other, or with the bank. Additionally, there are a fixed amount of resources which have value based on how badly the player needs them (for player to player transactions). For trading with the bank, there is a set ratio for trading that is by default 4 for 1. This ratio can change based on gameplay aspects such as ports. There is no metaeconomy.

Identify the important pieces of information. Which ones are hidden and which are open?
  • Open Information
    • Layout of other players roads/settlements/cities
    • What resources you get from a particular space on the board
    • Where the robber is
    • How many victory points the other players have
  • Hidden Information
    • What resources the other players are holding
    • What strategy the other players are using

Describe the methods of control. Are they direct or indirect? Real-time or turn-based?
The game uses an indirect, turn-based control structure. For example, players participate when it is their turn, where they roll the dice. If they roll a 7, then the players resource capacity is indirectly controlled by limiting it to 7 or less. This method of control is indirect because the rules do not state that you cannot have more than 7 cards - you can have as many as you want. You should, however, attempt to limit the number of resources you’re holding else you run the risk of losing half of your resources.

Describe one significant feedback loop. Is it reinforcing or balancing?
One significant feedback loop is, as mentioned, the one where if a player rolls a seven, every player with more than 7 cards must get rid of half of their hand. This would be an example of a balancing feedback loop because it limits the number of resources a player can accumulate at any one time.

Game: 7 Wonders

Designer: Antoine Bauza

Released: 2010

Description:
7 Wonders is a card drafting game featuring ancient civilizations. Players share a limited subset of cards representing structures that they can build in order to help their civilization, and “Wonder” prosper. Different structures facilitate different winning strategies including trade, combat, and resource production.

Additional Information:
  • https://boardgamegeek.com/boardgame/68448/7-wonders


Chapter 3:
Invitation to Play:
The box art is very colorful and prominently features well known ancient wonders all within the same city area; the art is appropriate and intriguing. The game’s box also advertises that it’s appropriate for players age 10+ and only takes around 30 minutes, meaning it’s open to a fairly large demographic. The game has received a number of rewards and is lauded for the gameplay it provides.

Number of players: 2-7

Player roles:
Every player has the same basic role. However, each player is in charge of developing one of seven different ancient civilizations and its respective “wonder”, each of which offers slightly different rewards for completing building tasks and may inform the player’s strategy.

Player Interaction Pattern:
  • Multilateral

Objectives: The main objective is to be the player with the most “victory points” at the end of the game. Victory points are earned from building basic structures, building your “wonder”, battling others, and strategically developing your civilization to maximize the benefits of certain structure cards. This offers players numerous paths to achieve the same basic goal.

Procedures: There are three separate “ages”. During each age, each player starts with 7 randomly dealt cards. Each turn, they can use the resources they currently have or trade with others with construct the building on a single card in their hand. If they choose not to build or cannot build because they do not have the necessary resources, they may instead discard a card for currency. The types of structures available to build, their cost, and the benefits they offer the player vary depending on the age. After choosing a card, players pass their hand to the player next to them (CW or CCW depending on the age), and repeat the process until one card is left. The last card is discarded. At the end of each age, players may earn positive or negative “conflict tokens” from battle if anyone has built military structures. At the end of the third age, victory points are computed for all players based on their conflict tokens and structures they have built, and the player with the most wins.

Resources:
  • Clay
  • Ore
  • Stone
  • Wood
  • Glass
  • Loom
  • Papyrus
  • Coins

Sources of conflict:
  • Players may choose to build military structures during the course of the game. If a neighboring player’s military outpowers your own, they gain victory tokens (effectively victory points) while you lose them. The amount of points gained increases as the age increases, incentivizing players to keep track of their military prowess as they develop their civilization.
  • Additionally, since the pool of cards is shared, each player is able to directly influence the cards the player next to them will have in their next turn. This means that players not only have to think about building what is beneficial to them, but also what they may not want their opponent to have.

Boundaries:
  • Each player has their own personal board / building area to construct their civilization.
  • Unwritten agreement to follow the rules and procedures of the game.

Possible outcomes:
  • A single player has the most victory points and wins
  • Two or more players tie victory points and the player with the most coins wins
  • Two or more players tie in victory points and coins and both win (very unlikely)

Although the premise and locations are based on history, there is not much story outside of the premise. Players won’t experience story during the game, but may choose to portray one based on the cards they play.

World Building:
Players are tasked with the development of one of the seven ancient civilizations: Gizah, Olympia, Bablyon, Alexandria, Ephesos, Halikarnassos, or Rhodos. As players build the different types of structures -- civilian, military, treasury, commercial, guilds, or the “wonder” -- different resources, actions and strategies become available to them and can influence subsequent building choices. Although players can build their city in any way they choose, there are many cards that play off each other thematically and the game mechanics reinforce this notion by making it more lucrative to build structures that relate.

The game occurs over three separate ages, effectively rounds. Though there is no real story throughout the game, each age provides different types of structures to build: the structures become more expensive but also more powerful and interesting. This provides a sense of development over time.

As they build the structure, they place it on the “board” for all to see. At the end of an age, they will be rewarded positive conflict tokens (+1 to +5, depending on the age) for each neighboring player they overpowered, while the player(s) they defeated will receive a negative conflict token (-1).

Describe how you might change the elements of the system in order to significantly change the dynamics; what do you believe the new dynamics will be like? Will the game still be playable? Will it still be balanced?
It would be possible to significantly change the game dynamics by changing how players are able to use and acquire resources. Currently, once a player has a structure that produces a resource, they have access to that resource for future construction every single turn. If players instead drew cards or tokens and were able to accumulate and expend resources to build, it would become necessary to think about potentially keeping certain resources on-hand for future turns. As is, players would likely end up with an overabundance of resources since the build cost and trade system is not designed with this in mind; however, if the costs were increased slightly or there was a limiting factor on how many resources a player could hold at once, it would add a significant layer of complexity to resource management.

Describe the economy within the game (if there is one). Is there a metaeconomy?
The economy centers around construction resources. Resources are used to build structure cards that produce more or different resources for use each turn. Resources may be traded (shared) with neighboring players if they pay for their use. This encourages players to build structures that generate different types of resources, especially ones their neighbors may need.

Identify the important pieces of information. Which ones are hidden and which are open?
  • Open Information
    • The civilization/wonder each person has and what it produces and provides for developing it.
    • Similarly, the structures that others have built, what benefits they provide (resources, battle, trade price reductions, victory points).
    • The coins each player has.
  • Hidden Information
    • The cards you may choose from to build each round. At the start of an age, this information is hidden from others. However, since cards are passed between players after building, it’s possible to loosely keep track of what cards a player may end up with when the deck you have gets to them. This is especially important if there are fewer than seven players and you have the chance of seeing the same cards twice.

Describe the methods of control. Are they direct or indirect? Real-time or turn-based?
7 Wonders follows a turn-based indirect control model. Each turn, every player has the opportunity to build a structure which slightly alters the dynamics of the game and the options available to each player. Although players are in control of what they build and are able to enact their own playstyle throughout the game, the decisions available to them are heavily influenced by what others may decide to do and the current state of the game board.

Describe one significant feedback loop. Is it reinforcing or balancing?
As previously mentioned, certain structure cards relate to each other thematically. These cards have special “build chain” properties where if you have built the previous structure, you are able to build the next one at no cost. The chained structures offer even greater benefits, and you don’t lose the benefits of the original structure. This is a reinforcing loop.

Chapter 3:
Invitation to Play:
Starcraft the video game has an arcade and campaign mode and its expansions have a co-op and multiplayer mode.

Number of players: 2 to 6 players. Best 4 players

Player roles:
Players take control of the three distinctive races featured in the video games, the Terrans, the Protoss, or the Zerg, to engage in battle across multiple worlds in order to achieve victory.

Player Interaction Pattern: Each player controls a faction out of six, that belongs to one race out of three - Humans, Zerg, and Protoss. Each faction has a unique special victory condition, but all factions can also win through victory points that are gained by controlling special areas on some of the planets. Players build units and base upgrades with the resources they gather from the planetary areas that they control, and gain access to additional unit types through those upgrades.

Objectives: Each of the six factions can achieve victory by being the first to accomplish one of the following victory conditions: a) accumulate 15 conquest points, b) fulfill its unique special victory objective, or c) eliminate every other faction from play. If no faction has accomplished one these goals by the end of the game, the faction with the most conquest points wins.

Procedures:
  • Each turn consists of three phases: the Planning Phase, the Execution Phase, and the Regrouping Phase. In the Planning Phase, players take turns placing four of their Order tokens face-down on any planet where they have forces present (note, since the Orders are placed this way, the Orders placed latest are resolved first); these tokens are resolved in the Execution Phase. Game adjustments, including scoring, occur in the Regrouping Phase. There are three types of Orders that utilize resources:
    • The Build Order allows Resources to be spent on the purchase of workers (which gather Resources), transports (which allow movement of troops between planets), buildings (increasing the variety of units that may be purchased by the players), modules (upgrades for bases), bases (for units to deploy), and units. Units are generally associated with the building that they are trained in the video game (or, for Zerg players, the building that allows them to be spawned in the video game).
    • The Research Order allows players to spend Resources to add Technology Cards to their Combat Card Deck.
    • The Mobilise Order allows troops to be moved to the planet where the Order was issued. Troops may be moved from other regions on the same planet, or from adjacent planets.

Resources: For every planetary area that provides gas or minerals, there is a corresponding Resource card. When a player harvests a resource from one of his areas, he must place one of his available worker tokens on that area’s Resource card, indicating that one unit of that resource has been gathered this game turn. Should an area become “partially depleted” (due to force mining or for any other reason), a player must turn the corresponding Resource card from its normal side to its “partially depleted” side. Should an area become depleted, the resource card is removed from the game and placed back in the game box. (Such an area can no longer provide resources).

Sources of conflict: Starcraft the board game has too many units, and too many abilities, learning 30+ units appearance and abilities, if they can attack , land or transport becomes difficult to keep track of. There is no simplified version of this game, learning strategies take a couple of turns playing the board game to understand and execute.

Boundaries: Players are bound by physical space they play in, as in the board game requires players to be in the same space in order to be able to play it, unlike video games particularly multiplayers games. The board game also restricts or forms the boundary of the game.

Possible outcomes: Three possible outcomes of a skirmish are: a) both players must destroy a unit, b) neither player destroys a unit, or c) one player must destroy a unit. The team with the most total conquest points (the sum of the conquest points from both players on the team) is the winner.

Resource Cards Each planet consists of several areas, each of which provides either conquest points, mineral resources, or gas resources to the player who controls the area. When a player harvests a resource from one of his areas, he must place one of his available worker tokens on that area’s Resource card, indicating that one unit of that resource has been gathered this game turn. Should an area become “partially depleted” (due to force mining or for any other reason), a player must turn the corresponding Resource card from its normal side to its “partially depleted” side. Should an area become depleted, the resource card is removed from the game and placed back in the game box. (Such an area can no longer provide resources). Achieving victory in SCBG is as much about resource management as it is about combat. To properly decimate their foes, players must strengthen their forces by building new units and upgrading their bases. Players must also strengthen their economies by building more workers, increase their mobility by building transports, and gain a technological edge over their opponents by purchasing Technology cards. Players accomplish these things by executing Build and Research orders.
Combat Cards Fighting in StarCraft is mainly about combat cards. This makes the combats calculable, but you need to have the right card on your hand. When two factions engage in combat, Combat cards are used to resolve the battle. Each faction has its own deck of Combat cards, from which that faction draws its hand of Combat cards. Card attack values and abilities vary from race to race. While accumulating resources, upgrading bases, researching technologies, and building new units are all important parts of SCBG, a player’s prospects for victory depend heavily on how effectively he uses his units in combat.
